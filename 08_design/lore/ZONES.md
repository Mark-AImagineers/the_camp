# The Zones
### *The world outside the walls*

---

The Camp sits at the center of a small, known world.

Nobody has mapped far beyond a few kilometers in any direction — not because there's nothing out there, but because distance is a resource too, and resources are scarce. What the Camp knows, it knows from expedition reports, from what traders have said passing through, from the occasional survivor who arrived from somewhere else and described what they left behind.

Four zones. All reachable on foot. All worth something. All dangerous in their own specific way.

The rule, passed down from before you arrived and written nowhere officially but understood by everyone: *you do not go alone, you do not go unprepared, and you do not stay past dark.*

Nobody wrote down what happens if you break the third rule. Nobody needed to.

---

## The Crossing
*Urban. Commercial strip. Close.*

A stretch of road that used to connect two parts of the city — shops, a small mall, a fuel station, a clinic, a row of restaurants that all had the same plastic chairs. The kind of place nobody thought about because it was always just there, always open, always lit.

It isn't lit anymore.

The Crossing is the Camp's most reliable scavenging ground and its most dangerous. Close enough to reach in under an hour on foot. Dense enough to find almost anything — medicine from the clinic, canned goods from the convenience stores, fuel from the station if you know which tanks haven't been drained or contaminated. Every basic resource the Camp needs has passed through The Crossing at some point.

The Altered density is high. Not the highest anyone has seen, but high enough that no one goes alone and everyone who goes comes back changed in some small way they can't quite name. It accumulates. The people who run The Crossing regularly carry something behind their eyes that the others don't.

What unsettles people isn't the Altered. It's the mall.

The small mall at the center of The Crossing — three floors, a collapsed section on the east side, a food court that still smells faintly of cooking oil — has been marked off-limits since before you arrived. Nobody from the Camp goes past the ground floor. The standing order, in Mark's handwriting, says simply: *second floor and above, no exceptions, no reasons given.*

Nobody has asked loudly enough to get an answer. Nobody has gone up to find one.

The hope is stubborn and specific: the pharmacy on the ground floor has a locked back room. Whatever is in it hasn't been taken yet because nobody has found the right key. Lena thinks about that room more than she admits.

---

## The Yard
*Industrial. Logistics hub. Three kilometers east.*

A former logistics hub — warehouses, loading docks, a vehicle depot, a fuel storage facility, a rail siding that hasn't seen a train in four years. The infrastructure of movement, frozen in place.

The Yard is ugly and useful. Sheet metal, heavy machinery, cable, tools, vehicle parts — things Darro needs constantly and can never get enough of. It's also exposed in a way that urban zones aren't. Wide open spaces between structures. Long sight lines that work both ways. Nowhere to disappear quickly if something goes wrong.

The Altered behavior here is different. In The Crossing they move the way you'd expect — reactive, toward sound and presence. In The Yard they've been observed standing still. Not dormant. Still. Facing the same direction. Three separate expeditions have reported it and three separate expeditions have quietly disagreed about which direction they were facing when it happened.

Tomás has a theory he won't share yet. He's waiting for more information. He's been waiting for a while.

There's a vehicle in the depot — a truck, older model, heavily modified with what looks like custom plating — that nobody has been able to explain. It wasn't in any earlier inventory. It arrived, or was placed, or was driven there by someone who hasn't been identified. It's locked. The fuel gauge, visible through the window, reads just above a quarter tank.

Whoever left it there may still be in the area. That could mean anything.

## The Lowfield
*Rural. Former farmland. North, past the overgrown road.*

Past a stretch of road the weeds have mostly reclaimed, the land opens up into what used to be agricultural territory. Fields, irrigation channels, a scattering of small structures — storage huts, a pump house, a small chapel at the edge of a property that must have belonged to someone once.

The Lowfield is the only place near the Camp where things still grow. Not cultivated — wild, overgrown, reclaimed by whatever decided to move in when the farmers left. But edible things grow there. Fruit trees in the wrong rows. Root vegetables seeded from whatever was left behind. A stretch of ground near the chapel that produces something consistently enough that Sera has it marked on her resource map with a small star.

The fear here is quieter than The Crossing. The Altered presence is low. What the Lowfield has instead is a stillness that reads as attentive rather than empty — the feeling of being watched from the middle distance, from somewhere you can't locate. Nothing has ever acted on it. It may be nothing.

The chapel is locked. Through the window you can see that someone has been using it — recently, consistently. Candles burned down to different lengths. A blanket folded on a pew. Marks on the wall that could be a calendar or could be something else.

Whoever is staying there hasn't introduced themselves to the Camp. They leave before expeditions arrive and the signs say they always come back.

The hope of the Lowfield is the most basic one available: if the land still grows things, survival is possible in a way it isn't when you depend entirely on what you can find. Darro has said, once, quietly, that with the right materials and enough time, the Camp could expand toward the Lowfield. He said it like he meant it. He hasn't said it again.

---

## The Line
*Wilderness. Forest. West, past the edges of everything.*

To the west, past the edges of what anyone would call inhabited territory, the land rises and thickens. Forest. Not managed, not cleared, not interrupted by roads that go anywhere useful. Before, it was the kind of place people drove through to get somewhere else.

Nobody goes to The Line for resources. The yield doesn't justify the distance or the risk. People go because they have to — because something went that way, or someone said something was there, or because they've run out of options in every other direction.

What comes back from The Line comes back quieter. Not Altered. Not physically changed. Just quieter in a way that other survivors recognize without being able to describe. The ones who've been deep in say the forest is too silent — not the silence of a place where nothing lives, but the silence of a place where everything in it has learned not to make sound.

No one has reported Altered in The Line. That should be reassuring. It isn't.

There are structures in there — not ruins, not abandoned buildings, but deliberately constructed things that predate the collapse by what looks like decades. Shelters. Caches. A system of markings on trees that follows a logic nobody has decoded. Someone built infrastructure in that forest before any of this happened, for reasons that made sense to them, and whoever they were, they understood something about what a world like this one would eventually require.

The hope buried in The Line is the largest and the most dangerous: there may be people in there who know things. About the Altered. About what happened. About what is still happening.

Nobody has found them.

Or if they have, they haven't come back to say so.
