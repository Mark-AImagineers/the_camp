# The Survivors
### *The people inside The Camp*

---

Nobody counted them on purpose.

That's not how it happened. People arrived, people stayed, people took up space in corners and along walls and eventually in the rhythm of a place that was becoming, without anyone deciding it, a home. Sera keeps the list. She updates it when someone arrives and she updates it when someone doesn't come back. She doesn't share the number readily. When you ask, she says *enough that we can't afford to lose anyone* and looks at you until you understand what she means.

The count is somewhere between twenty-seven and thirty-two, depending on the week.

Not all of them will talk to you. Not yet. Some are waiting to see what kind of Coordinator you are. Some are waiting for a reason. Some are waiting for something to happen that unlocks the part of them that trusts.

You'll learn them over time. That's the only way anyone learns anyone here.

---

## The Inner Circle

*These are the ones who keep the Camp running. They were doing it before you arrived and they'll keep doing it regardless. The question is whether they do it with you or around you.*

---

### Sera
**Late 30s. Before: schoolteacher.**

She taught children how to read. Comprehension, retention, attention to detail — she brought all of it with her and turned it into something the Camp could use. She keeps the ledger, the headcount, the inventory, the names of people who didn't make it back. She does it without being asked because someone has to and because letting things go unrecorded feels, to her, like a second kind of death.

She trusted Mark completely. Which means his disappearance hit her in a way she won't show in front of people. She'll work with you. She'll be professional about it. But there's a question behind her eyes every time she looks at you — *are you going to disappear too?*

She knows where everything is. She also knows things about the Camp's history she hasn't volunteered yet.

---

### Darro
**Mid 40s. Before: construction foreman.**

Big. Quiet. The kind of man who communicates more with what he doesn't say than what he does. He built most of what makes the Camp functional — the reinforced gate, the rain collection system, the section of wall that stopped an incursion eight months ago. If it's standing and it's holding, Darro either built it or fixed it.

He didn't like Mark. Doesn't bother hiding it. Doesn't say why. He'll do the work, he'll follow reasonable orders, but he watches you the way you watch a structural beam you're not sure about — waiting to see if it holds.

He's the one people go to when they don't want to come to you. That's either a resource or a problem, depending on how you handle him.

---

### Lena
**Late 20s. Before: nursing student. One semester short of finishing.**

She's not a nurse. She'll tell you that herself, clearly, the first time someone calls her one. She knows enough to be dangerous in the good way — she can clean wounds, manage infections if the supplies hold, recognize the things she can't fix and say so honestly. What she can't do is pretend she has answers she doesn't have, and she won't.

She came to the Camp alone. Doesn't talk about what happened to the people she was with before. She's good at her job and she keeps a careful distance from everyone, which the Camp respects because good medical knowledge is rare enough that nobody wants to make the person providing it uncomfortable.

She was neutral on Mark. She's neutral on you. To her, leadership is something that proves itself or it doesn't.

---

### Tomás
**Early 50s. Before: police officer. Retired.**

Retired two years before everything fell apart. He'd been spending his time fixing up an old motorbike and growing vegetables. Neither of those things prepared him for what came next, but thirty years of reading people and staying calm in bad situations did.

He's the closest thing the Camp has to security — not because he volunteered but because people started deferring to him in tense moments and it became a role by default. He accepted it the way he accepts most things: quietly, without drama, with full awareness of what it costs.

He respected Mark the way you respect someone competent. Not warmth — respect. He's extending you a provisional version of the same. What he won't tolerate is recklessness with people's lives. He's lost enough already.

He's one of the people Mark told things to. Not everything. But things. Some of it Tomás will share when he trusts you enough. Some of it he may take to his grave.

---

### Yssa
**19 years old. Before: student. She was still in school.**

She was fifteen when the world ended. The Camp is the only adult life she's ever known. That makes her both the most adapted person here and the most quietly furious about it — she doesn't mourn a career or a life she built because she never got to build one.

She's fast, observant, and better in the Gray Zones than people twice her age because she learned in the After, not carrying over habits from Before. She runs expeditions better than most. She doesn't take orders well from people who haven't proved themselves to her personally, and she makes no apology for it.

She never met Mark — arrived after he'd gone. Which means she has no loyalty to his memory and no patience for people who use it as a yardstick. She'll measure you on what you do. That's either the fairest deal in the Camp or the hardest, depending on your decisions.

---

## The Broader Camp

*Known faces. Names on Sera's list. Their depth unlocks through time, events, and the choices you make.*

---

### Cullum
**60s. Before: doesn't say.**

Old enough that people assume he's fragile. He isn't. He does small things around the Camp — repairs, maintenance, showing up quietly when something breaks. Never asks for anything in return. Rarely speaks. When he does, people listen, though nobody could explain exactly why.

He knew something about Mark that he's decided isn't his to share. Whether that changes depends on circumstances nobody has created yet.

---

### Raine & Otto
**Raine: early 30s. Otto: her son, seven years old.**

Raine does whatever work keeps Otto safe. That is her entire operating logic. She's not difficult and she's not warm — she's efficient in the way that grief makes people efficient, all the softness redirected into function.

Otto doesn't talk much. He draws. The drawings are of things no seven-year-old should have seen, rendered in whatever materials he can find, left in corners of the warehouse like he's trying to put them somewhere outside himself.

---

### Felix
**Mid 30s. Before: mechanic.**

The only person in the Camp who fully understands the generator. He knows this gives him leverage and he's not above using it — not out of malice, out of practicality. He wants more food allocation for the work he does. He thinks that's fair. Whether you agree shapes the relationship.

He's not wrong that the Camp runs partly on his goodwill. He's also not wrong that goodwill has limits.

---

### The Three

*A group of men, late 20s to mid 30s, who arrived together. They work. They don't cause problems. They don't integrate. Nobody knows their full story and they haven't offered it. They have a dynamic between them that suggests a shared history nobody else was part of — a shorthand, a way of moving around each other, an awareness that reads less like friendship and more like people who've been through something together and came out changed.*

*Tomás watches them more than he watches anyone else in the Camp.*

---

### Rook
**Early 30s. Before: unclear. He says logistics.**

The scar runs from his left ear to his jaw. He'll tell you he got it from a car accident. He'll tell you differently each time. He doesn't care if you believe him — the deflection is the point, not the story.

He's the one the other two defer to, though none of them would describe it that way. He makes decisions quickly and without visible doubt, which is either confidence or something he learned to perform. He works hard. Never complains. Never volunteers for anything either — waits to be asked, then does it well enough that you can't complain about the waiting.

He knows things about what happened in the early days that he files under *not relevant anymore*. Whether that's true is a question he's decided isn't yours to ask.

---

### Marsh
**Late 20s. Before: doesn't say. Watches the gate.**

He's the one you notice standing by the perimeter when he should be asleep. He doesn't pace — he stands, still, facing out, like he's waiting for something specific. Nobody's asked him directly what he's waiting for. The people who've thought about it decided they don't want to know.

He's quiet in a way that's different from Darro's quiet. Darro is still because he chooses to be. Marsh is still because something trained him to be. He follows Rook's lead without it looking like following. He does his work. He eats alone.

He's useful on runs — good instincts, moves well, doesn't panic. But there's a flatness to how he handles danger that makes people uncomfortable in a way they can't quite name.

---

### Ivo
**Mid 30s. Before: says he was a teacher. Smiles when he says it.**

The smile is the thing people notice. It's easy, warm, the kind of expression that belonged to the Before — to someone who sold things, or hosted things, or made people feel comfortable for a living. In the After, that kind of ease reads as a mask or a weapon, and most people at the Camp have settled on one or the other without deciding which.

He's charming. He remembers names after hearing them once. He offers to help before being asked. All of which would be fine if it didn't feel, to certain people, like a performance that's been running for a very long time.

Tomás doesn't trust him. Sera finds him useful but keeps the ledger locked when he's nearby. Yssa thinks he's funny, which worries Tomás more than anything else about him.

---

### Bridget
**Late 40s. Before: accountant.**

She keeps a personal ledger that isn't the Camp's official one. She's never explained why. She's methodical, reliable, and has strong opinions about resource allocation that she will absolutely share if asked and sometimes if not asked.

She disagreed with Mark on at least two major decisions. She'll tell you which ones if you ask. She's not telling you to undermine him — she'll be clear about that. She's telling you because she thinks you should know the decisions that were made and why she thought they were wrong.

Whether she's right is something you'll have to figure out yourself.

---

### The Children

*Four of them. Various ages. Various situations. Otto belongs to Raine. The other three arrived in different configurations of alone. They are the reason certain decisions feel heavier than they would otherwise. Nobody says this out loud. Nobody has to.*

---

### Alma
**8 years old. Attached herself to Sera in the first week.**

She follows Sera through the warehouse with the focused quiet of a child who has learned that the safest place is near competent adults. She doesn't ask questions. She hands things over before Sera asks for them. She's memorised the inventory system better than half the adults and corrects mistakes by silently moving items back to where they belong.

She doesn't talk about where she came from. She doesn't talk much at all. When she does speak, it's practical — quantities, locations, what's running low. Sera has stopped trying to get her to play. What Alma does with the ledger *is* play, as far as Alma is concerned.

She sleeps on a mat next to Sera's cot. Nobody assigned this. Nobody questioned it.

---

### Juno
**12 years old. Insists on working.**

She showed up alone, walked through the gate carrying a bag that weighed more than she did, and asked who was in charge of work assignments. That was her first sentence at the Camp. Her second was *I don't need a bed, I need a job.*

She's stubborn in a way that nobody has found a good argument against, because every argument requires telling a twelve-year-old that she's too young to be useful, and she'll look at you with an expression that makes the word *young* feel like something that belongs to a world that doesn't exist anymore.

She does maintenance. She carries water. She sorted the medical supply closet in a single afternoon and Lena hasn't been able to improve on the system since. She refuses to be treated as fragile and she's earned it, which is a sentence that shouldn't apply to someone her age but does.

Her older sibling is somewhere in the Camp. Barely managing. Doing their best. Juno doesn't talk about it.

---

## The Rest

There are others. Faces in the morning. Names on Sera's list that you haven't matched to people yet.

Some are recovering from things — injuries, losses, the particular exhaustion of having survived something that took everything else. Some are simply not ready to be known yet. Some are watching to see what kind of Coordinator you are before they decide whether to exist in your direction.

The full count is somewhere between twenty-seven and thirty-two, depending on the week, who came in, and who hasn't been accounted for.

Sera knows. Ask her directly and she'll say: *enough that we can't afford to lose anyone.*

She'll look at you when she says it.

She does that on purpose.
